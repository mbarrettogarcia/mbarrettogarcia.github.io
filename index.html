About

I am a Postdoctoral Research Associate in the Department of Neuroscience at Washington University in St. Louis. My primary research interest is understanding the neuronal mechanisms that underlie value encoding across complex economic choice tasks, including trinary choice, risky choice, and intertemporal choice.

I completed my PhD in Neuroeconomics at the University of Zurich. I also completed an MRes in Behavior and Cognition at the University of Barcelona, and an MSc in Economics and Finance at the Barcelona School of Economics. I received my undergraduate degree in Economics and Statistics from the University of San Carlos in the Philippines.

My intellectual trajectory has been shaped by generous mentors and advisers. My current postdoctoral adviser is Camillo Padoa-Schioppa. My PhD adviser was Christian C. Ruff. My MRes thesis adviser was Josep Marco-Pallarés, and my MSc thesis adviser was the late Robin Hogarth.

Research

Individual risk attitudes arise from noise in neurocognitive magnitude representations

Nature Human Behaviour, 2023

This project examines why individuals differ in risk attitudes. Rather than treating risk aversion only as a feature of subjective utility, the study links risky financial decisions to the precision of magnitude representations. Using psychophysics, computational modelling, and fMRI, the paper shows that noisier magnitude representations can contribute to stronger apparent risk aversion.

Causal evidence for a domain-specific role of left superior frontal sulcus in human perceptual decision-making

eLife, 2026

This project asks whether the left superior frontal sulcus supports decision-making in a domain-general way or whether its role is specific to perceptual decisions. Combining fMRI, computational modelling, and transcranial magnetic stimulation, the study provides causal evidence that the left superior frontal sulcus contributes selectively to perceptual decision-making.

Neuronal Activity in Orbitofrontal Cortex during Trinary Choices under Risk

Journal of Neuroscience, 2026

This project extends the study of orbitofrontal value coding from binary choice to trinary risky choice. Monkeys chose among three offers that varied by juice type, quantity, and probability. The study examines how OFC neurons represent offer values, chosen value, choice outcome, probability, and spatial variables when the choice set contains more than two options.

Other Work

Alongside my scientific work, I pursue projects in philosophy, economics, constructive systematic theology, and poetry. These projects ask how cognition, value, judgment, form, and institutional life can be understood across disciplinary boundaries.
